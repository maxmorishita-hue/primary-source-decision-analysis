# Primary-Source Decision Analysis (45-minute lesson)

This repository provides a classroom-ready lesson designed to help students
analyze historical decision-making using primary sources.

## Purpose
- To practice reading primary sources critically
- To identify constraints, assumptions, and decision inputs
- To explain outcomes without relying on moral judgment

## Target
- Upper secondary school students (advanced)
- Adaptable for middle school with simplification

## Key Features
- Uses authentic primary sources (Japanese & English)
- No fixed conclusion required
- Evaluation based on reasoning process, not opinion

## Lesson Length
- One class period (45 minutes)

## Topics Covered
- Diplomatic decision-making
- Constraints and limited options
- Interpretation of official documents

This material is intended for educational use and does not promote any
particular historical interpretation.

## Source
"Source A Japan 1941"
Japanese diplomatic communications indicate increasing pressure from
resource shortages, domestic political expectations, and time constraints
in negotiations with the United States.

"Source B US Hul Note"
The Government of the United States and the Government of Japan will not
support any arrangement which would violate the territorial integrity
or political independence of any nation.

## worksheets/student_worksheet
1. List the constraints mentioned in Source A.
2. List the demands or conditions in Source B.
3. Which constraints appear incompatible?
4. What outcomes became more likely as a result?
5. What information is missing or unclear?

## assessment/evaluation_rubric
Evaluation Criteria (No fixed answer required)
- Correct identification of constraints (40%)
- Use of evidence from sources (30%)
- Logical explanation of outcomes (20%)
- Clarity of reasoning (10%)

## appendix/teacher_QA_and_risk_notes
Q: Is this a political lesson?
A: No. This lesson focuses on document analysis and decision-making processes.

Q: Are students required to take a position?
A: No. Students are evaluated on reasoning, not conclusions.

Q: Why use English sources?
A: To expose students to original wording and reduce interpretive bias.
